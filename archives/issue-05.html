Jukebox


N°5 | 10 May 2020

Hello,

And welcome to another issue of Finding Beauty, a weekly collection for the curious, the self-motivated learners and the explorers inside us all, edited by Antonio, our Creative Director and Head of Storytelling.

How was the first bite of freedom? One morning I had to go out and there were so many more people around, but everyone with masks and socially distanced. It was encouraging. Be part of that courage and not of those who throw these two months of isolation like a ball between nettles.
Because then it is up to us to go and pick it up, with short pants and flip flops. In short, enjoy freedom responsibly.

This new Top 5, however, enjoy it out of all proportion, boundlessly, irresponsibly. Enjoy it in a gargantuan way. Open all the links at once, spam them, then close them again and look at them sufficiently. Treat them with respect or with contempt. With approximation or with meticulousness. Do what you want. Beauty is what you make of it.

[Remember that you can contribute to Finding Beauty too, by pointing out all that you find beautiful on the Internet. Just send an email to findingbeauty@imille.agency]

 

#5

 

Visual effects by Nature®️

I've always said it: nature is the best production house. Think about how many expensive films have tornado scenes inside (at this point there are two types of people: those who are now endeavoring to remember as many tornado films as possible and those who lie). Including those with shark-filled tornadoes. But the way nature designs the storyboard and then shoots it, still remains unique.

And if there are still doubts about it, look at this. And blow them away.


#4

 

Colors for the grey times we’re living in

This post starts like most corona-commercials: now more than ever there is a need for colors, after the 60 shades of grey of the quarantine days. If it's a little color you are looking for, here's a whole Pantone chart. These are the works of the Mathery Studio. Scrolling through their portfolio is like witnessing an accidental explosion of a paint factory.

Without anyone getting hurt of course. Except maybe your eyes.
 

Mathery Studio


#3

 

Mask-erade


What Yrurari does is like watching someone eating a pizza, starting with the crust: annoying and irresistible. At the exact center between the serious and the facetious. But when something, even crocheted, manages to convey so many sensations, it means that it has great communicative power.

Things that are made to cover the mouth, like its latest masks, are capable of speaking a lot.
  
yrurari


#2

 

You’re welcome, maniacs friends

I probably inherited the obsessive tendency to order from my father. Partly because as a tailor, precision was his professional deformation; partly because he's just like that. The garage of the house where I grew up was the ideal space for the release of his drives. There he could pigeonhole objects in a Tetris of studied combinations, the result of continuous improvements; and then look at it with Michelangelo's satisfaction in front of the Pietà.

Michael Johansson's works are somewhat reminiscent of my father's garage. And if you are also a maniac of things well-ordered, like books being arranged from largest to smallest or apps grouped on your smartphone by color, you're welcome, maniac friends.

  
Michael Johansson


#1

 

Last night an AI saved my life

Maybe in the future, we will have to rewrite songs like the one to which the title alludes, given that, apparently, artificial intelligence is capable of writing its own. More than "writing" them, AI knows how to "remix" them, just like a DJ does. You put the data about genres, times, lyrics, etc. and it remixes them. The debate on the relationship between what’s "natural" and artificial intelligence returns with the same punctuality of my neighbor using the drill on Saturday afternoon, just after lunch.
And it's a bit like those debates related to how the invention of the radio would have replaced the printed paper and then how the TV would have replaced the radio and then finally how the Internet would have replaced the TV. And then all of a sudden you are holding a smartphone with which you can read, listen to the radio, watch TV and go on the Internet.

The fact is that this Jukebox project is extremely interesting.

And maybe it will end up a bit like the smartphone, a convergence of media: with Pharrell Williams singing falsetto the texts written by the system, Daft Punk creating vocoder effects on the bases and all of us dancing to the music throughout the whole summer.



 


Antonio Di Battista
