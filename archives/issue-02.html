Hello,

And welcome to a new issue of Finding Beauty, a newsletter powered by Imille. A weekly collection for the curious, the self-motivated learners and the explorers inside us all.
Five absolutely arbitrary doses of content, in the form of an absolutely arbitrary Top 5, once a week, selected by Antonio, our Creative Director and Head of Storytelling.

The story of this newsletter started a few years ago, with a series of “and then”. Instead of sending random things to his creative team to get it inspired, Antonio started pasting the links into a draft email. And then to send it every week. And then he started building a narrative framework around it. And then he realized that the search for sources of inspiration and, ultimately, beauty, was the founding theme of this framework. And then, one request of addition after the other, he came to send the email to hundreds of people. And then, years later, those hundreds became thousands: you.

Remember that you can contribute to Finding Beauty too, by pointing out all that you find beautiful on the Internet. Just send an email to findingbeauty@imille.agency.

Shall we start?

#3

 

Opening titles to point out

It is certainly not as bright as The Loudest Voice, with an unrecognizable Russell Crowe as the founder of Fox News. But The Morning Show, another series with an extremely similar plot, impressed me for the nice motion graphic work in the opening titles. There are more beautiful ones, of course — Freud, The Crown, Leftovers, Westworld and the unparalleled Game of Thrones, just to name a few.
We could almost say that opening titles are a genre within the genre. So why mention this one from The Morning Show? Because they started from an essential shape like a point and managed to tell, through a cool work of minimal design and visual metaphors, the movements, and narrative that develop between the characters, throughout the entire series. In short, if you think that sometimes the opening titles are better than the series, well, you have a point.

#1

 

The beauty of emptiness

Probably the best definition of greatness is the ability to see wonder, where others see nothing or even see ugliness. If this assumption is true, then the photographer Romain Veillon is not great. He is a giant.
With his ability to tell stories, where others would only see emptiness and abandonment. With his talent in kidnapping the soul of places that were already looted. With his inability to stop telling them.
Please take a look.
