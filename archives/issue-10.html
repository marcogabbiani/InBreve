Hello,

And welcome to another issue of Finding Beauty, a collection for the curious, the self-motivated learners and the explorers inside us all, edited by Antonio, our Creative Director and Head of Storytelling.

You can read all the previous issues here. 

In this article on Medium, we speak of the urgency of seeking beauty in a world that seems to do everything to push us towards its opposite. We talk about how beauty has been an evolutionary concept throughout history and how often its canons have changed. About how all this is the breath of Finding Beauty, in the fascinating double meaning of the gerund of "to find": looking for beauty, but at the same time the possibility that beauty is looking for us.

Let's be clear, it's always a wonderful thing when it happens, but it's better to be the first to research it. Not as amateur guitarists who strum when they can, but as virtuosos of scales, riffs, chords and improvisations, because we cannot risk losing the harmony of beauty.

In a world of cheap musicians, be Vahid Iran Shahi.

#5

 

I could have done it too

This is the title of a beautiful book by Francesco Bonami, a critic who writes about contemporary art with a prose that flutters like a chat with a stranger at the bus stop. The concept of the book is simple: "I could have done it too" is the feeling we sometimes feel looking at the products of contemporary art.

A blank canvas, a gash and voila: I could have done it too. Yet that canvas and that gash - meticulously obtained - are the result of a research path that places Lucio Fontana among the highest steps in the history of art. Whatever your opinion on the subject, works of art with the simplest ideas are often the most longevous ones. Just like Fontana's gash.

Looking at the works of Jean Paul Donadini you have at first glance a feeling similar to that described by Bonami. But if you give a closer look at this sentence which summarizes its narrative, his ideas soon enter the circle of universal simplicity: J'aime l'idée d'une surface plate qui devient dynamique.

Maybe I could have done it too. But he did it, and I did not.


#4


Our New World is Theirs

The beautiful documentary on Franca Sozzani has the merit of telling us about the role that Vogue has had in the construction of fashion in the collective imagination.

Operations such as the one I'm going to tell you about reinforce that role: "The new issue of Vogue Italia, out on June 9, is dedicated to children. For the first time in the history of the magazine, in fact, children draw eight covers of the issue. There is a simple and fundamental reason why this issue of Vogue Italia is dedicated to them. It is because we think they are the great removed, the least obvious victims of the pandemic that hit us all. Without even considering the injury to the right to play and socialize, it is simply incredible and unacceptable that in many countries, including ours, it is not yet known whether and how schools will reopen in September."

A way to remind us all that we only have the world on loan and that soon we will have to return it to the next generation. A way to remember that our world is theirs.


#3

 

Running away from the city

The generations going up to the Millennials onwards will probably remember this cult film from 1991. In the comedy's plot, Billy Crystal escapes from the oppression of the metropolis, seeking some comfort among the cows of a ranch, reproducing the centrifugal tendency that urbanized history has produced over the centuries, and which recent lockdowns have accentuated.

Alex Ohgalé is an American photographer who has made a series of shots dedicated to escape: escape from chaos, noise, everyday life. The perfect combination of neutral settings, essential clothing and the measured movements of the models, is capable of giving observers a feeling of quiet, calm, and unusual serenity. Precisely the same way that you feel when running away from the city.
 


#2

 

It's just stuff

Billions of investments in brains and media in the capitalist world, to ask people for one thing in return: buy my stuff.
Maybe that's why I am very impressed by minimalism and the approach of Hedvig, a home insurance brand which does not explain the tranquility of being insured with the usual 30-second commercial that teaches about how peaceful you are when you are insured with them (with a fake dad looking at the camera while strapping shin pads on his fake son who is going to play soccer, accompanied by the notes of cheerful tune bought from a stock music library).

No, Hedvig makes you experience serenity. With the minimalism of the direction, the delicacy of the voice over, the choice of situations and shots. And above all, with the message: where everyone says "buy my stuff", they say "it's just stuff". After all, even if they will be destroyed, they are just objects.

Here you can find their playlist of movies.
One more beautiful than the other.
These are of value. More than the stuff itself.


#1

 

Wenders meets hopper

Made for the exhibition of the Fondation Beyeler in Basel - unfortunately closed due to the Covid-19 emergency - the short film that Wim Wenders dedicates to Edward Hopper looks very promising. As if Hopper's pictorial tales did not already have enough depth, another master of the image like Wenders (author of one of the most beautiful documentaries of the last 10 years, The Salt of the Earth) applies the technique of 3D shooting to make the stories of Hopper more vivid than ever.

The short film, entitled Two or Three Things I Know about Edward Hopper, aims to immerse the viewer in the paintings of the American artist, recreating some of his most famous scenes. Akira Kurosawa had already tried to bring us inside the works of an artist, Vincent Van Gogh, in that masterpiece which is Dreams, but Wenders' work makes a mirror path: bringing the work of the artist to us.

Undoubtedly, one of the best things that happened to me this week.
