Hello,

And welcome to another issue of Finding Beauty, a collection for the curious, the self-motivated learners and the explorers inside us all, edited by Antonio, our Creative Director and Head of Storytelling. 
[If you want to contribute, point out all that you find beautiful on the Internet, by sending an email to findingbeauty@imille.agency]

You can read all the previous issues here. 

This latest issue before the summer break summarizes the eclecticism that this newsletter has always promised to have. You will find content that comes from social media, the visual arts, communication, cinema, and culture in general. 

Each time the calendar tells us the year ends on December 31st, but we know that the end of July is a more truthful mark of the real end of a year. If that’s true, then let’s wrap it up nicely and put some good intentions in our hearts, according to tradition. 
There never was, as much as in this 2020, the desire that everything ends as soon as possible, and on this insight Burger King has built a brilliant communication stunt. 

I hope Finding Beauty has kept you company over the weekends and given you a few moments of lightness, inspiration and simple beauty to get you through the week ahead. These will be the holidays of the absence of plans. Many will get in the car without a specific destination, but only for the need to travel. Then try to rediscover the pleasure of boredom, ataraxia, otium - why not - even literary. 

Lose yourself in nature like the child of Ermanno Olmi’s beautiful Terra Madre and rediscover the names, flavors and shapes of flowers and plants like Adam and Eve in your own personal Eden. 

And contrary to what we have suggested several times in this newsletter, just let beauty find you for once.
 

#5
CREATIVITY MAKES YOU HAPPY


He’s 83 and she’s 84 — and they model other people’s forgotten laundry.⁣ Chang Wan-ji and Hsu Sho-er have become Insta-famous for wearing outfits curated from the hundreds of items left behind by absent-minded customers at their laundromat in central Taiwan. Their 31-year-old grandson, Reef Chang, originally came up with the idea to create @wantshowasyoung on Instagram when business slowed down because of the coronavirus pandemic. “They had nothing to do”, he said. “I saw how bored they were and wanted to brighten up their lives”.⁣ Turns out, they were naturals in front of the camera. Sho-er exudes the haughtiness of a supermodel but retains an air of playfulness. Wan-ji is the perfect foil, complementing his wife’s swagger with a chill disposition while rocking bountiful eyebrows.⁣

The couple now receives messages from fans all over the world. Wan-ji said he’d be happy if the hundreds of people who have forgotten to pick up their laundry would return to pay their bills.⁣ Sho-er added: “Creativity has made us happy, and other people, too.”⁣ 

Creativity can make you happy, too. You choose whether to wait up to 84 years or try to give it a chance today.
 

#4
TRANSFIGURATION

 


It is a reworking of the Universal Everything studio classic from 2011. The Transfiguration was first shown at the studio’s first major solo exhibition Super-Computer Romantics at La Gaite Lyrique, Paris. Now completely remade using the latest procedural visual effects software, the updated artwork brings new life to the ever-evolving walking figure, with a new foley-based soundtrack by Simon Pyke. 

In other words, start watching it and then try to quit, if you can.
 

#3
A PROMISE FOR ETHAN


“Apple is carbon neutral. But that’s not enough. Apple reached that goal in April 2020, and we’re certainly proud that our facilities, corporate emissions, and corporate travel don’t contribute carbon to the planet. We use 100 percent renewable electricity, and we’ve invested in the restoration of forests, wetlands, and grasslands to remove carbon naturally. However, all of this is just a starting point. We have an entirely different goal in mind.

It’s kind of an audacious plan. By 2030 our whole carbon footprint - from manufacturing to transportation to end-of-life material recovery - will be nonexistent. All of Apple will be 100 percent carbon neutral. To do that, we’ll be using renewable energy and recycled materials throughout our supply chain. We’ll be restoring natural ecosystems around the world. And we’ll reuse as much as we can. While designing the world’s most innovative products, we’re also designing a sustainable manufacturing process.

To really get to carbon zero while making products people all over the world love - a lot of people might say that’s impossible. We say it won’t be. Keep an eye on us and see”. 

In other words, a promise for Ethan and his entire generation.
 

#2
DRAWING A LINE


Ethologists have observed that drawing a line prevents ants from following their path, forcing them to follow the line. Do we feel like ants in front of a line drawn on our horizon? Can this line change the way we look at the horizon itself?
This is the thesis that the documentary Borderlands tries to develop.

“It isn’t so fascinating that the simple act of drawing a line on a map can transform the way we see and experience the world?” Ronald Rael observes in the opening of the documentary.
The US started installing border fences in the early 1990s and, in the past decades, the wall has only grown taller and longer — separating everything along its way, including rivers and Native American territories, cultural sites and universities, people's homes and wildlife preserves. 

Borderlands attends to the reality of living in a community that straddles two cities.
 


 

#1
THE HUMAN LIBRARY


In ancient Egypt, the place where they were supposed to be born, libraries were called “The treasure of soul remedies” because they cured the worst of evils: ignorance. Attracted by the popularity of these treasures, a historian named Hecateus had left Greece to visit Egypt. Astonished, he stopped in front of a portal on which stood an inscription translating as follows: Place of care of the soul. What place was it? Hecateus had the answer when he entered it: it was the sacred library of Ramesses.

Can libraries still be considered places of healing from ignorance even today? In Copenhagen they believe so and there, in fact, the Human Library project was born. Libraries where you can “borrow” a person, instead of a book, to hear the story of her/his life. A library of humans, where you can “read” a person for half an hour to help you fight prejudice. Each person has a deliberately direct title: “unemployed”, “lesbian girl”, “Islamic woman with veil”, “Albanian emigrant”, to make it clear how wrong it is to judge a book by its cover. 

In Bradbury's Fahrenheit 451, each person was associated with a book so that their memories would not be lost. Here people don't memorize books, people are the books.
If Marina Abramovic had speculated that having a person in front is enough to activate an incredible flow of emotions, imagine what a person in front of you can do when they present you their life story.
 


 

Finding Beauty greets you for the summer break, with a quote from Mémoires d'Hadrien.

“The founding of libraries was like constructing more public granaries, amassing reserves against a spiritual winter which by certain signs, in spite of myself, I see ahead…”
