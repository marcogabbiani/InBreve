N°16 | 26 July 2020

Hello,

And welcome to another issue of Finding Beauty, a collection for the curious, the self-motivated learners and the explorers inside us all, edited by Antonio, our Creative Director and Head of Storytelling. 
[If you want to contribute, point out all that you find beautiful on the Internet, by sending an email to findingbeauty@imille.agency]

You can read all the previous issues here. 

From some recent studies, it seems that seasickness is a tool that nature has invented to remind us of the danger of venturing out into deep water, especially when it’s rough. It's a bit like a fever: an intelligent defense mechanism. A sensation comparable to what the world is experiencing in the stormy year of 2020.

Yet, if the Ennio Flaiano’s quote is true - "the situation is difficult, but not serious" - then a certain dose of irony can help us navigate it.

The line that unites this issue is what geometry calls a circumference arc. The same shape that goes from one end of your mouth to the other when you smile.

I tried to collect a few things that could help us find a bit of lightness (that of Italo Calvino's American Lessons, according to which "lightness is not superficiality, but gliding on things from above, not having boulders on the heart") to guide us through these dark waters.

Float on this new uplifting Top 5 that now comes lightly in the heart and with a dose of Dramamine in the pocket.
 

#5
THE OPPOSITE OF OLDENBURG


Claes Oldenburg's thesis sets out to prove that even an everyday object, brought on a large scale, can become a piece of art. Many of his works were born from this philosophy, including the giant needle and thread that adorns the Cadorna station in Milan. But what happens when things are reproduced on a very small scale? I believe the same grotesque and ironic result is obtained, but “concave” rather than “convex”.
The film Downsizing even wonders what would happen if the whole scale of mankind was reduced.

Chris Toledo does this with historic houses. "Each house is the result of a process that can last for years and which begins with research and in-depth study, followed by sketches, floor plans, 3D models and preparation of a real miniature construction site".

Whether it's zooming in or out indefinitely, whether it's Oldenburg or his opposite, it's dedication to the process that increases the curve of hyperbola and irony.
 


 

#4
HUMAN FEELINGS WITH NO HUMANS IN IT


With a colorful and irreverent figure, and a world of rubbery materials, soft latex or fluids that somehow escape Newton's laws, Zolloc tells us about a vast variety of human emotions, without ever resorting to a single representation of humans.

You could almost define them as animated comic strips, but they are much more. They are sometimes beautiful metaphors, like this, they are pieces of history or even simply beautiful things to appreciate for their look and technique.

It's almost funny to think how much humanity there is in this Instagram profile and how much, sometimes, you miss in so many others.
 

#3
ART IS IN THE HAIR


There is no space that is not a space for expression.

Laetitiaky reminds us once and for all, using her hair like the canvas of a painter, the empty balloon of a cartoonist or the blank page of a novelist. That allows her, from time to time, to write whatever story she wants.
Often with a comedy plot.
 


 

#2
THAT JE NE SAIS QUOI BY JACQUEMUS


Jacquemus is a fashion brand that has accustomed us to see extraordinary things. After the beautiful walkway among the rows of lavender in southern France, it’s back with a new performance in nature for the SS21 collection.

In the brand's narrative, nature becomes an essential story line. Not as a stage, but as one of the main characters, whose events unfold and twist in an endless narrative arc. There is, however, an ironic je ne sais quoi in bringing the ethereal beauty of the models and the naivety of fashion among wheat fields that are a symbol of agricultural evolution, scythes, bloody battles, peasant struggles and divisionist painting.

Maybe it's a way to recover a little lightness in the relationship with those fields, on which our livelihood has depended for centuries and on which our future depends.
 

#1
IF BIRDS HAD ARMS


I don't think anything more fun can happen to you this week. The profile of Duncan Evans is one of the possible antidotes to seasickness we were talking about in the beginning.

In his bio it’s written: "I used to milk cows. Now I make videos".

As if that’s not enough to love him already, here is one of his latest, hilarious creations that spread on social media faster and more furious than an Australian wildfire during the dry season.

The creative mechanism is apparently simple: adding an element into an alienating context. And then there is the incredible execution, the careful choice of clips, the accurate post production.

Demonstrating, once again, that making people laugh is a serious thing.
 


 

Have a safe trip and enjoy these beautiful souvenirs.


 


Antonio Di Battista
