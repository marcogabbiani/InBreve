N°9 | 7 June 2020

Hello,

And welcome to another issue of Finding Beauty, a collection for the curious, the self-motivated learners and the explorers inside us all, edited by Antonio, our Creative Director and Head of Storytelling.

Slowly but surely, we’re finally starting to emerge from our Covid-19 cocoons. Some of us have reunited with friends and family over a socially distant dinner, served with a side of sanitizer. We’ve warmed the seats of our local bars to indulge in a much-needed cocktail, or two, after a days worth of attempting to make comprehensible conversation behind our facemasks. Some have even managed to grin and bear the lines outside the shopping centers in order to fulfill the noble purpose of supporting the local economy. Meanwhile, many of us are also returning to the streets because, in the US, the tradition of unnecessary police brutality has reared its ugly head yet again. But nothing says "welcome back to normal life" quite like the level of air pollution reverting back to exactly the same pre-pandemic extremes, even though schools and most offices are still closed.

It was said that from this experience we would come back renewed, stronger, smarter. Some brands have even hailed it as "a new day of a rediscovered humanity". I hope they are right.

In the meantime, here is a new injection of beauty with which you can refresh yourself and maybe, why not, also find some ideas on how to be human again and to put the kind back in humankind.

#5

 

Right under Your feet

"In the cellars of the Italians, there is a treasure". This sentence is attributed to Giulio Carlo Argan, one of those intellectuals we’re in need of today, to help us understand what is happening around us. Argan's sentence refers to the fact that often in the cellars of our grandmothers there are design pieces - ranging from the 20s to the 70s - which we often pay a lot of money for to furnish our houses, when instead they are just thrown there, covered with dust and munched on by moths. And sometimes even more ancient treasures, such as sculptures, parts of columns, paintings and other incredible finds can be excavated from our basements. Well, apparently Italy is full of treasures even lower than our cellars, and this latest find is a brilliant example.

If this happens by just scratching the surface, think about what we might find if we dig a little deeper. Things that perhaps not even Jules Verne in his Journey to the Center of the Earth could have imagined.


#4


Zero faces, tons of stories

The world of the painter Gideon Rubin is a world without faces, if not actually devoid of humanity. Canvases where eros, romance, everyday life, detail and crowds intertwine seamlessly.

But for some reason that I can't explain, I find these faceless quadrilaterals vivid, vibrantly expressive and rich in narrative.
 


#3

 

No photographer, no problem

Gucci is a brand that is increasingly deserving of its place in our Top 5. Their communication is proving to be fresh, lively and capable of finding intelligent answers to changing times.

What I would call their "Selfie Collection" is one of these answers.

Not the only operation of its kind that we have seen during the lockdown phase, but certainly one of the most well done.


#2

 

The time spent on the search for beauty is never wasted time

"Photographer Rachael Talibart used telescopic lenses and an ultra-fast 1,000 frames / second shutter speed to capture the towers and troughs of foam-flecked seawater during Storm Imogen in February of 2016. You probably haven't seen waves like this before."
Dive into the story of how the photographer got these incredible shots and how his determination was crucial for the result. Some would say that waiting for hours, days or even years for the perfect wave is just as much a waste of time for a surfer as for a photographer. But without this waste there would be no such shots, nor stories like Point Break. (The first, of course, not the embarrassing remake)

You can also find her on Instagram. A high-profile profile.


#1

 

How to be human

In one of our speeches during the Milano Digital Week, we talked about the need for humankind to re-brand itself, to rethink what it means to be part of humanity with a new language, a new system.

I think this campaign for the Brooklyn Film Festival expresses conceptually well the thesis we set into motion with that video and at the beginning of this week’s Top 5: it's time to go back to being human.

Maybe there is a need for new guidelines, since we have forgotten how to do it. If so, try to consider this campaign as a tutorial to inspire. A mix of powerful insights, excellent execution and beautiful graphic design.



 


Antonio Di Battista
