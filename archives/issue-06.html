N°6 | 17 May 2020

Hello,

And welcome to another issue of Finding Beauty, a weekly collection for the curious, the self-motivated learners and the explorers inside us all, edited by Antonio, our Creative Director and Head of Storytelling.

How's the new normal going for you? Call it Stockholm Syndrome. Call it the old story in which you see the tunnel through the eye of an interior designer, when you can't get out it. Call it whatever you want, but hey, I'm starting to get used to this crazy journey and I’m even defining new balances along the way. The human being needs to find routines. Maybe we are simply starting to replace the old with the new ones. And perhaps the new routine will consist of periodically defining new routines.

I hope beauty can become part of the new routine that we are creating. Tautologically, the most beautiful part. Not necessarily the supporting column, but at least an architrave supported by baroque, luxuriant capitals.

I feel that the engines of ugliness are reigniting: rivers that return polluted in just one day of productive recovery, people who return after 18 months of captivity judged with the jurisprudence of social media, etc etc. (The list could go on).

So equip yourself with the antidote of beauty, without looking at the doses. Take these 5 or more pills and take the only substance that improves you. Make beauty your new addiction.

#5

 

Gucci Street View

We still can't go to museums, as you know. But then again, Gucci allows you to take a virtual tour of his own, located at the Daelim Museum - an exhibition space in the Gucci Places group. A project that aims to support Seoul's rich cultural landscape and contemporary art scene.

Take a ride, it's worth it.


#4

 

Virtual food is so real

What would food and all the other elements be like if they did not respond to the laws of nature?
Lukas Vojir's works seem like a list of attempts to answer this question. If it were up to him, Isaac Newton would have never discovered anything, because his magenta apple would have flown in space and dissolve in a cloud, instead of falling on his head.

  
Lukas Vojir


#3

 

Is there a border between artist and work of art?


If Antony Micaleff were to answer this question, he would probably say "no". It is the feeling that I have looking at his work: that for him the canvas is a constriction and, if he could, he would also paint the sky if only the color was enough. But not like a painter does. More like a child who scribbles a sheet very strongly, imagining himself drawing precise shapes but ending up out of the paper. With a vibrant, primordial instinct.

Maybe that's why his work seems borderless.
  
Antony Micallef


#2

 

Playing with the physics rules

The director Paul Trillo knows that there's gravity as well as other principles which, according to the scientific community, regulate the universe. But you know, this is the same scientific community that burned Giordano Bruno in 1600 AD or that excommunicated another man (whose name I do not remember) because he had understood that worms, insects and dozens of small critters were reproducing like the humans and not growing spontaneously in the mud like common belief.

In short, director Paul Trillo knows that these rules exist. He simply doesn't care about them and bends them to his liking, like Nolan's Paris in Inception. Immerse yourself in this world without rules.

  


#2B

 

Tweets worth revisiting

Influencers are undoubtedly one of the many entrepreneurial figures who are suffering from the lockdown. Lots of brands and agencies are receiving contacts and proposals from them, much cheaper than some time ago. A sign that the influence is starting to drop, just like the Covid-19, hopefully.
Burger King, in its endless genius, used them in an even cheaper way and made one of the creepiest and cringiest things that you can do on social media look cool: "Have you ever had someone like your Facebook posts from years ago? It's super creepy. It takes a long time to scroll to get to those pictures. It's obvious they're shuffling through all of your pictures. It can give one the heebie-jeebies."

This is the whole story, and is a must-read.


#1

 

Fall in love with cinema again

There are relationships that don't need weekends out, gifts or romantic dinners, small jealousies and skirmishes to stay alive. The one between me and the cinema is one of them. The first two films that I saw, and that I remember as if it were yesterday, are Beauty and the Beast and Dance with Wolves. Both films with beasts as protagonists, actually. That cinema casted a spell to me and after more than 30 years, I am still a happy victim.

If by chance you feel a little tired or are living one of those moments like "it's a bit that I don't see exciting things", well, I suggest you dive into the Vimeo profile of Jorge Luengo Ruiz with soft lightning and pop corn.

And get ready to fall in love with cinema forever.
 



 


Antonio Di Battista
